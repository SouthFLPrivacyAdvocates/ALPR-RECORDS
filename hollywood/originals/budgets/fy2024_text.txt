Reader’s Guide to the Adopted
Fiscal Year 2024 Operating Budget
The Adopted Fiscal Year 2024 Operating Budget serves as a communication tool
for defining strategic priorities and organizational structures; identifying sources of
City revenues; and analyzing Department and Office operating expenditures, program
objectives, and performance measures. It also shows how economic and financial
forecasting affects decisions in the areas of long-term financing, capital investment,
program organization and services offered. These elements are essential for sound
management of public resources.
This section describes the structure and components of the Fiscal Year 2024
Operating Budget to enhance reader understanding and facilitate use of the
document. The Budget has been organized in a manner that is consistent with the
National Advisory Council on State and Local Budgeting and the Recommended Budget
Practices which requires the reader be presented with a guide to government
programs and organizational structure. These sections are consistent with the
Government Finance Officers Association’s standards for the Distinguished Budget
Presentation Award.
Special Chapters
The special chapters provide general budget information at a high level to
inform the reader of the City’s policies, organizational structure, budget process,
component funds and staffing. This section is composed of Hollywood at a Glance,
Introduction, and Summary of Funds.
Hollywood at a Glance
This section provides the reader with some quick details about the City:
• Citywide Organizational Chart • Community Profile
• Strategic Plan at a Glance • Service Data
• Hollywood Report Card • City Profile
Introduction
This chapter contains the following information:
• Budget Overview • Budget Development Process
• Staffing Overview • Budget Calendar
• Assessed Value & Millage Data • Operating Policies & Procedures
• Impact on Homeowners • Citywide Strategic Plan

October 26, 2023
City of Hollywood, Florida
Honorable Mayor, Vice Mayor, and City Commissioners
Re: Fiscal Year 2024 Adopted Operating Budget & Capital Improvement Plan
Dear Mayor, Vice Mayor, and City Commissioners:
I respectfully submit the Fiscal Year (FY) 2024 Adopted Operating Budget and the associated
five-year Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) requests for FY 2024 – FY 2028 for the City of
Hollywood.
The FY 2024 Adopted Operating Budget is structurally balanced meaning the City’s ongoing
revenues sufficiently cover ongoing expenditures without the use of one-time funds.
Fiscal Year 2024 Taxable Values & Millage Rate
The Citywide certified taxable value for FY 2024 totals $23.2 billion, which represents an
increase of 11.18% over the prior year’s final adjusted value of $20.9 billion. This year marks
the second year of consecutive double-digit growth. Over five years, the citywide taxable
value grew 35% ($5.9 billion). This year’s growth has allowed us to:
• Reduce the total millage rate by 0.0702 mills (from 8.1548 mills to 8.0846 mills).
• Advance many of the City’s strategic goals in the areas of resiliency and sustainability,
public safety, financial management and administration, employee empowerment, and
quality of life. This is in addition to the many initiatives and programs that we currently
have in place.
• Finally, this budget also invests over $70 million in non-recurring resources for critical
capital projects such as street resurfacing, new playground equipment, stormwater
improvements, and facility improvements.
Although the City’s tax base has grown significantly over the recent years, it is important to
note that the annual increase in assessment for homesteaded properties cannot exceed the
lower of the following: 3% of the increased in assessed value of the property for the prior year
or the percentage change in the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for all urban consumers.
Similarly, non-homesteaded properties are capped at a 10% annual increase of assessed
value.

Key Priorities
In recent years, we have endured a global public health crisis, unprecedented natural
disasters and climate events, increased cybersecurity threats, social unrest, and other public
safety concerns. These trends are further complicated by global security threats that will
inevitably affect our City, residents, visitors, businesses, and employees. For these reasons,
resiliency and sustainability, public safety, and cybersecurity are my top priorities for FY 2024
and beyond.
Below are the key initiatives approved in the FY 2024 Operating Budget and Capital
Improvement Plan:
Resiliency and Sustainability
• Create a Chief Resiliency Officer position to properly resource, elevate and
advocate for the integration of resiliency and sustainability; and mitigate the
impacts of climate change
• Dedicate $150,000 for a Net Zero Consultancy to identify, create, and acquire
resources and programming to develop a roadmap and pathway to the City’s
future that is stable, sustainable, and compatible with the changing
environment
• Fund maintenance of existing dunes and set aside a contingency for the City’s
share of new dune construction managed by Broward County (in the event of
cost overruns)
• Renew and retain the City’s certification of environmental stewardship through
the Florida Green Building Coalition
• Encourage compliance in making Hollywood Beach more hospitable for sea
turtles through the promotion of the Sea Turtle Lighting Recognition Program
Public Safety
• Add 8 sworn Police Officer positions (4 of which will be funded and dedicated
to the Beach and Downtown CRA districts)
• Convert a vacant Code Manager position to a Director of Public Safety position
to elevate the leadership and resources of inter-organizational coordination
between Police, Fire-Rescue, Beach Safety, Public Health, Emergency
Management, and Personal Safety
• Enhance public safety efforts with additional armed security personnel
throughout the beach and Broadwalk, increase funding for a replacement fire
engine and other fire rescue apparatus
• Increase the use of technology and automation with Closed-Circuit Televisions
(CCTVs), License Plate Readers (LPRs), Radar Speed Detection, etc.
• Increase funding for 6 part-time School Resource Officer positions
• Convert 25 part-time Lifeguard positions to full-time positions

Economic Update
The U.S. economy can best be described as weird and wild. The global equity markets and
the stock markets reported strong returns during the first quarter of the year, however, the
market has defied expectations leaving some experts to believe that a shift in a bull market
is quickly underway. From the real estate standpoint, we experienced unprecedented activity
in the residential market which largely contributed to the growth in citywide property values.
Likewise, according to Visit Florida, the state has drawn over 33 million visitors during the
second quarter of the year (which is 1.2% fewer tourists compared to the same period last
year). Although slightly lower than the prior year, the massive number of tourists has
positively contributed to the growth in other revenue categories for the City such as sales tax
and parking revenues.
Despite these optimistic reports, fears of an economic recession within the next 12 months
remain relatively strong. These fears are, in part, driven by relatively flat employment figures,
the yield curve remains inverted, deepening selloff of U.S. bonds, and the lingering effects of
the Federal Reserve’s aggressive monetary policy.

Factors Impacting the Budget
Of the many economic indicators described in the previous section, affordable housing (or
lack thereof) remains to be a top concern for the City and its residents. The affordable
housing crisis is systemic throughout the U.S. cities (with rentals growing 7.9%). However,
the rate of increase is much higher in South Florida compared to other parts of the country.
In addition to affordable housing, there are other macro and micro-economic factors that we
must continue to monitor and take proactive steps as needed including (but not limited to):
• Impacts of climate change and natural disasters
• Ongoing labor shortages particularly in hard-to-fill positions
• Unfunded mandates and other state legislation further limit local government’s ability
to generate additional revenues
• Disruption in the manufacturing of certain materials and equipment (such as HVAC
systems) resulting in lengthy lead times
• Security threats domestically and abroad and
• The cooling down effect in the real estate market

I look forward to a year of service enhancements, completion of capital projects, and
implementation of other initiatives for FY 2024.

The City of Hollywood, Florida
It’s all about location and Hollywood has it – in the heart of South Florida between
Fort Lauderdale and Miami. As the 3rd largest city in Broward County, it’s easy to
understand why Hollywood is both a sought-after tourist destination and a perfect spot
for doing business. The City’s founder came to Florida in the 1920s to create a “dream
city” where people could “make a living and live while making it.”
Hollywood is at the crossroads for international trade with easy access to major
employment centers, key transportation routes and is readily accessible by sea, air and
land. The region’s primary transportation resources include Interstates 95 and 595, the
Florida Turnpike, Tri Rail commuter rail, and two major railroads, all of which run
through the City. Hollywood is adjacent to Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International
Airport and is the primary home to Port Everglades, one of the top cruise ports in the
world and one of the most active containerized cargo ports in the United States.
Port Everglades
Miami International Airport and the Port of Miami are less than 25 miles away,
providing additional opportunities for Hollywood residents and companies to access the
global marketplace.

with industry best practices. Examples of these policies, practices, and procedures

 Formalize revenue estimates generated by a Revenue Estimating Committee,

Property Tax Revenues
On July 1, 2023, the Broward County Property Appraiser’s Office certified the
City’s taxable property value at $23,286,087,150 which is $2,341,155,491 or 11.18%
above the previous tax year’s final gross taxable value, after corrections, of
$20,944,931,659. For FY 2024, the City proposes an operating millage rate of 7.4665
mills which is the same as the previous year’s millage rate. This operating millage rate
is projected to generate $167,020,947 in net property tax revenues for FY 2024,
inclusive of statutory discounts, uncollectible taxes, delinquent taxes and penalties.
Fire Rescue Assessment Revenues
The Fire Rescue Assessment Fee will increase from $304 to $312 per residential
unit. The FY 2024 assessment covers 97.5% of the assessable cost of fire rescue
services provided to City residents and businesses, generating net revenues of
approximately $30.1 million.
Fire Inspection Fee Revenues
The Fire Inspection Fee will remain at $19 for inspected residential units and is
expected to generate approximately $2.7 million.
Community Redevelopment Agency (CRA)
In FY 2024, the reimbursement to the City for enhanced services from both
districts of the CRA and prior year set aside per the Inter-Local Agreement with
Broward County is approximately $10.0 million.

General Obligation Bond (G.O.B.) Capital Improvement Projects
In November 2004, voters approved a two-part referendum supporting the
issuance of a General Obligation Bond for Public Safety and Parks & Recreation capital
improvements. Prioritization and scheduling of projects, along with the initial
selection of design and engineering agencies for larger projects began in FY 2005. This
borrowing was refunded July 2015.
In March 2019, voters approved three questions for the Hollywood on the GO
Bond which provided funding to improve public safety facilities, purchase technology
and equipment and improve the City’s parks and cultural facilities. The first tranche of
debt in the amount of $70 million was issued in the fall of 2019 and the second
tranche totaling almost $95 million was issued in the fall of 2022.
The required debt payments for the three bond issuances total approximately
$13.7 million for FY 2024 and will be funded by the voted debt service millage rate of
0.6181 mills, which is a decrease of (0.0702) mills below the FY 2023 voted debt
service millage rate of 0.6883.
GASB 75 - Other Post-Employment Benefits (OPEB) Requirement
The line item for “OPEB Cost” is an important component of the budget
considering its extensive impact on the City’s fiscal condition. Each proprietary fund
includes an expense line that represents the anticipated cost of post-employment
benefits other than pension costs, as determined by an independent actuary. This
change was initiated by the Government Accounting Standards Board (GASB) to
provide more complete information on obligations incurred by state and local
governments. The City offers healthcare as a post-employment benefit.
The implementation of GASB 75 required the City to record total OPEB liability,
which eliminated the previously reported net OPEB obligation (GASB 45). The City’s net
OPEB liability decreased from $564,350,282 as of September 30, 2021 to $334,203,137
as of September 30, 2022. This significant decrease is largely due to 1) change in the
discount rate, 2) decrease in claims experience, and 3) changes in trend. Contributions
to post-employm ent healthcare for the City’s governmental funds remain on a “Pay-As-
You-Go” basis.

Operating Policies & Procedures
Budget Policies
The development of the Operating Budget is guided by the following policies:
 The primary budgetary objective is to provide the highest possible level of
service to the residents of Hollywood without impairing the City’s financial
condition.
 The budget must be balanced for each fund; total projected revenues must
equal total anticipated expenditures.
 The City will avoid making budgetary decisions that balance the current year’s
budget at the expense of meeting future years’ obligations.
 The City will maintain a budgetary control system to ensure adherence to the
budget and will prepare quarterly projection reports which will analyze actual
revenues and expenditures to budgeted amounts.
 Annual budgets are legally adopted for the general fund, proprietary funds, and
certain governmental funds.
 Laws and policies on limitations to revenue sources must be explicitly
addressed in the budget process.
Basis of Budgeting and Accounting
The annual budget, which represents departmental appropriations, is prepared
on the modified accrual basis utilizing encumbrance accounting. Encumbrances are
used to indicate the “intent to purchase goods or services,” which does not need to be
received for liabilities and expenditures to be recorded.
In brief, the modified accrual basis of accounting allows for revenues to be
recognized when susceptible to accrual (measurable and available). Measurable means
the amount of the transaction can be scientifically determined. Available means
collectible within the fiscal period or subsequently to be used to pay liabilities of the
current period.
The Enterprise Funds (Water & Sewer, Sanitation, etc.) on the other hand, are
budgeted on a full accrual basis. Expenses are recognized when incurred. Revenues
are recognized when they are obligated to the City. For example, water and sewer fees
are recognized as revenue when bills are issued.

Budget Adoption
The City and its component units’ fiscal year begins on October 1st of each year

Annually, the City Manager submit to the City Commission a proposed budget
for the upcoming fiscal year based upon Commission established priorities which align
with the Strategic Plan. Upon receipt of the proposed budget, workshops and one-on-
one briefings are held with the City Commission. The Commission may make
modifications by consensus of its members; however, no votes are taken at a
Commission workshop. Prior to the final adoption of the budget, and in accordance
with State Statutes, the Commission holds two public hearings where the public is
given an opportunity to make comments. During these hearings, the Commission may
again revise the budget by majority vote. On or before the thirtieth day of September,
the City Commission adopts the budget and property tax rate by the affirmative vote
of at least a majority of its members. Upon final adoption, the budget is in effect for
the ensuing fiscal year and becomes the authority for the various departments and
offices to expend funds subject to controls established by the City’s Comprehensive
Budget Policy and Florida Statutes.
Budget Amendments
From time to time, it may become necessary to modify the adopted budget for
various reasons. Generally, the budget may be amended in one of two ways:
administratively via a budget transfer or through a budget amendment requiring
Commission approval.

Administrative Budget Transfers
Administrative budget transfers include transfers from one line item to another
within a department’s operating budget, within a capital project, or changes between
divisions within a department. More specifically:
 Budget transfer requests of $15,000 or less require approval by the department
head and authorization by the Director of Budget; and
 Budget transfers between $15,001 and $100,000 require the previously stated
approvals as well as the Director of Financial Services and City Manager’s (or
City Manager’s Designee) approval. The transfers, however, must be made
within the same office or department within a distinct fund. Where the budget
transfer is the result of the creation of accounts and transfer of funds
necessitated by the creation or amendment of a division within a department or
the creation or amendment of a special office within the Office of the City
Manager, the City Manager (or City Manager’s Designee) may approve the
budget transfer without Commission action. All administrative budget
transfers are documented and tracked in the City’s financial management
system.
Commission Approved Budget Amendments
Budget amendments requiring City Commission approval are those amendments
which result in a change to the total appropriations of a department or fund and/or
exceed the City Manager’s approval threshold. The following amendments require
Commission approval
 All budget transfers over $100,000
 The acceptance and appropriation of a grant award
 The appropriation of additional funding if expenditures are projected to exceed
budgeted amounts
 Any budget transfers and adjustments within a sixty (60) day period following
the close of the fiscal year, per Florida Statute 166.241
 Adjustments to reflect unanticipated revenues or receipts; and
 Transfers of appropriation from one department or fund to another.

Balanced Budget

 It is appropriate to use excess unrestricted fund balance for one-time

budget, fund balance cannot be used as a long-term approach to balancing the
budget. Any use of fund balance which would result in a remaining fund
balance less than the City’s established minimum seventeen percent reserve
must comply with the requirements of the City’s Fund Balance Policy.

peripheral expense associated with the service.
 The tax rate must be adequate to produce sufficient revenues to cover City
services approved by the City Commission.

Long-Range Planning
The purpose of the long-range financial planning policy is to establish strategic
fiscal sustainability and resiliency principles for the City which shall be used in the
annual budget development process and to assist with making critical financial,
economic development, and community development decisions.

and external issues that may fiscally impact the City. Long-range financial planning
enables the City to take proactive steps to mitigate known issues within its control.
This is a mechanism to safeguard the City’s finances for current and future City of
Hollywood residents. To accomplish this, the City prepares a financial forecast as part
of the annual budget process. The financial forecast will be for a period of 5 years.

City.

Use of One-Time Revenues
The City will not use one-time revenues to finance ongoing programs. To the
extent feasible, one-time revenues will be applied only towards one-time expenditures.
Furthermore, one-time revenues will not be used for non-recurring expenses that may
result in future year obligations such as debt, staffing, and other anticipated operating
cost expenditures.
Use of Unpredictable Revenues
The City has established a Revenue Estimating Committee that meets
periodically to review revenues, formalize estimates, and establish projection
methodologies. The Revenue Estimating Committee shall identify revenue sources that
are considered unpredictable. Since these revenue sources are volatile and unreliable,
the Revenue Estimating Committee shall define how these revenues may be used (in
adherence to the One-Time Revenue Policy), analyze the degree of volatility, and
project a minimum base for each revenue. As such, any portion of these revenues
collected that is above the established minimum base shall be treated as one-time
revenue. This policy shall be evaluated each year during the budget process and
documented in the City’s adopted budget book.

Overview of Funds
The City follows the Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) as part of
the budget and accounting processes. These principles require that the City use
separate accounting entities called funds to keep track of the resources used for
different purposes. There are two (2) different classes of funds: Governmental Funds,
which include the General Fund, special revenue funds, debt service funds and capital
projects funds; and Proprietary Funds, which include the enterprise funds and internal
service funds.
Governmental Funds
This category includes those individual funds through which most
governmental functions are typically financed.
General Fund (001) – The fund used to account for all financial resources, except
those that are required to be accounted for in another fund. Most governmental
operations such as police and fire protection, recreation activities, and planning and
zoning administration are accounted for in this fund. Ad Valorem property taxes are
also budgeted in this fund.
Special Revenue Funds (111, 113, 115, 116 and 164) – The City uses special
revenue funds, considered non-major governmental funds, to account for specific
revenues that are legally restricted to expenditure for specific purposes. These funds
relate primarily to revenues and expenditures associated with federal, state, and local
government grant programs. Expenditures for these purposes are dependent upon
receipt of related revenues.
Building Fund (114) – This special revenue fund is used to account for revenues and
expenditures related to enforcement of the Florida Building Code including review of
building plans, building inspections, and permit processing.
Debt Service Fund (223) – This fund is used to track the payment of principal and
interest related to long-term debt used by the City of Hollywood. These instruments of
debt may include special obligation bonds, bond anticipation notes, and tax
anticipation notes.

Fund Balance / Net Position
Net position is the difference between an entity's assets plus deferred outflows
of resources and its liabilities plus deferred inflows of resources. Within the
governmental funds, this difference is known as fund balance. This accumulation of
unexpended revenues may be reserved to provide a financial cushion in the event of
unanticipated reductions in revenues, sudden economic downturn and to cover a
reasonable level of extraordinary unbudgeted expenditures.
The City maintains two (2) months of General Fund operating expenditures in
reserves (approximately 17%). This budgeted reserve target is a portion of the
unassigned fund balance that is reflected in the City’s annual financial report. The
City’s Annual Financial Reports may be found on the following page:
http://www.hollywoodfl.org/538/Annual-Financial-Pension-Fund-Reports.

Five-Year Forecast
The Five-Year Forecast is a tool used for long-range financial planning and
budgeting purposes. It is a series of estimates – informed estimates, but estimates,
nonetheless. The five-year forecast is beneficial in several important ways: (1) identify
potential issues in advance and devise solutions rather than waiting for problems to
evolve and (2) maintain reserves in the General Fund at the target level of seventeen
percent (17%) of total expenditures.
There are many variables involved in the development of a multiple year
financial forecast, and several assumptions for each of those variables, on both the
revenue and expenditure side. There are many unknown variables, for example, the
rate of future growth in the assessed valuation of taxable properties.
Based on the Five-Year Forecast presented in this document, there is one
valuable conclusion that can be drawn: the City, similarly to many local governments,
continues to face the challenge of the annual growth in expenditures outpacing the
annual growth in revenues. In recent years, the Office of Budget & Performance
Management (Budget Office) in collaboration with City Departments and Offices has
taken steps to improve the financial outlook of the City. A Comprehensive Budget
Policy was established to guide City Departments and Offices in managing budgetary
resources responsibly. Additionally, a Comprehensive Schedule of Fees was established
to provide a more organized method for review and revision of fees during the annual
budget process. Furthermore, the Coronavirus Local Fiscal Recovery Fund, established
under the American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA), has been an instrumental resource
following the economic impacts stemming from the pandemic. Although a one-time
resource, the ARPA funds have helped the City to address critical needs that would
have otherwise increased projected gaps.
In summary, the collaborative efforts of City staff along with significant growth
in taxable values contributed to the City’s ability to produce a structurally balanced
budget for FY 2024. However, it is important to note that the volatility in the current
economic environment will inevitably challenge the City’s ability to sustain a structural
balance in the outyears. For these reasons, the five-year forecast utilizes several
external sources when developing the assumptions. Some of the sources include the
Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) as projected by
the Congressional Budget Office (CBO); Florida Department of Revenue; the Office of
Economic and Demographic Research; and Broward County Property Appraiser. Major
assumptions are detailed on the following pages.

coordinates and manages all active, inactive, and archival records of the City. The

preserves, and makes available vital municipal and historically significant documents
and photographs of Hollywood.
