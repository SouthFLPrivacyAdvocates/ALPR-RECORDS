presented a Distinguished Budget Presentation Award to City of Hollywood, Florida, for

Reader’s Guide to the Adopted
Fiscal Year 2023 Operating Budget
The Adopted Fiscal Year 2023 Operating Budget serves as a communication tool
for defining strategic priorities and organizational structures; identifying sources of
City revenues; and analyzing Department and Office operating expenditures, program
objectives, and performance measures. It also shows how economic and financial
forecasting affects decisions in the areas of long-term financing, capital investment,
program organization and services offered. These elements are essential for sound
management of public resources.
This section describes the structure and components of the Adopted Fiscal Year
2023 Operating Budget to enhance reader understanding and facilitate use of the
document. The Budget has been organized in a manner that is consistent with the
National Advisory Council on State and Local Budgeting and the Recommended Budget
Practices which requires that reader be presented with a guide to government
programs and organizational structure. These sections are consistent with the
Government Finance Officers Association’s standards for the Distinguished Budget
Presentation Award.
Special Chapters
The special chapters provide general budget information at a high level to
inform the reader of the City’s policies, organizational structure, budget process,
component funds and staffing. This section is composed of the City Manager’s Letter,
Hollywood at a Glance, Introduction, and Summary of Funds.
Hollywood at a Glance
This section provides the reader with some quick details about the City:
• Strategic Plan at a Glance • Community Profile
• Citywide Organizational Chart • Service Data
• Hollywood Report Card • City Profile
Introduction
This chapter contains the following information:
• Budget Overview • Operating Policies & Procedures
• Staffing Overview • Budget Calendar
• Assessed Value & Millage Data • Citywide Strategic Plan
• Impact on Homeowners

Summary of Funds
The Summary chapter provides an overview of the FY 2023 Operating Budget by
fund. Each fund is identified according to category and purpose. Charts and tables
illustrate fund structure, five-year forecasts, revenue sources and history and
appropriations by fund.
Revenue Sources
This section details all City revenues, separated by fund and revenue type.
Department / Office Chapters
Each Department and Office chapter contains Department / Office and Program
level descriptions, appropriations, and staffing. In addition, the Department and
Office summaries contain accomplishments, goals, performance measures, and
appropriations by Fund and Division.
A Non-Departmental chapter is included to identify and describe the functions
of each non-departmental program. These programs do not contain staffing,
objectives, or performance measures.
Capital Improvement Plan
The Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) section defines and describes the sources
and uses of funds for capital improvements, as well as the process for approving
projects and appropriating funds. Narrative and charts are provided to inform the
reader on the following related topics:

October 19, 2022
City of Hollywood, Florida
Honorable Mayor, Vice Mayor, and City Commissioners
Re: Fiscal Year 2023 Adopted Operating Budget & Capital Improvement Plan
Dear Mayor, Vice Mayor, and City Commissioners:
I am pleased to present you with the Fiscal Year (FY) 2023 Adopted Operating Budget and
the associated five-year Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) for FY 2023 - FY2027 for the City of
Hollywood.
The City’s budget is an important communication device used to present the allocation of
resources to support ongoing service demands and advance the strategic priorities for our
City. The FY 2023 Adopted Budget represents a turning point for the City as this is the first
structurally balanced budget in over a decade. This accomplishment demonstrates your
continued commitment to being responsible stewards of City resources.
Fiscal Year 2023 Budget Overview
The City’s FY 2023 certified taxable value totals $21 billion, which is an 11.56% increase over
the prior year’s final adjusted value of $18.8 billion. Over five years, the citywide taxable
value grew 39% ($5.8 billion). This impressive growth is largely attributable to increased
economic development activity throughout the City.
This year’s growth in taxable values allows the City the opportunity to maintain a flat operating
millage of 7.4665 mills, eliminate the capital millage of 0.0145 mills, reduce the Series 2005
debt service millage from 0.2007 mills to 0.1806 mills, and reduce the Series 2019 debt
service millage from 0.2149 mills to 0.1934 mills. Lastly, this budget includes the Series 2022
debt service millage of 0.3143 mills for the final issuance of the voter-approved General
Obligation Bond debt.
The Citywide FY 2023 Adopted Operating Budget totals $672.5 million (gross). This
represents an overall decrease of $101.7 million or 13.1% compared to the FY 2022 Adopted
Budget. The decrease is related to the $100 million deep injection well project in the
Department of Public Utilities which was funded in FY 2022.

The Economic Landscape
Two years ago, as the spread of the COVID-19 pandemic peaked in the U.S., we ventured
into an economy filled with uncertainties. Following the reopening of businesses and the
distribution of vaccinations, the U.S. economy appeared to be on the rebound until this year.
However, 2022 has been a year of economic challenges. Russia’s invasion of Ukraine
resulted in significant impacts on global economic markets. The combination of an ongoing
energy crisis, climate change, rising inflation, and the residual effects of the pandemic
continue to hamper global economies.

Much like the rest of the world, the U.S. economy over the past ten months can best be
described as peculiar. As of the timing of this letter, the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for All
Urban Consumers and the Personal Consumption Expenditures Price Index (PCE) have both
increased by over 8% in the past 12 months. The hardest hit categories include energy, food,
shelter, and transportation (all of which directly affect consumers).
To combat inflation, the Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate by another 75 basis
points in September bringing the funds rate to 3% - 3.25% (the highest since 2008). To
further complicate matters, the stock market continues to decline with the third-quarter
performance of the S&P 500 down 16.8%.
On the flip side, the job market has improved with a half million more jobs added since the
pandemic, and the unemployment rate is down to 3.5%. However, the “Great Resignation”
continues to challenge many industries, particularly those in the service sector, with the total
number of separations at 3.9% in August.
Factors Impacting the Budget
The City’s revenue sources hardest hit by the pandemic, such as parking revenues, have
recovered to pre-pandemic levels. Additionally, the strength of the City’s tax base has played
a significant role in our overall financial sustainability. This is evident with the recent rating
agencies’ assessment of the City’s creditworthiness.
Based on the rating agencies’ analysis, the growth and expansion pf the City’s tax base,
maintenance of healthy financial reserves, relatively low debt burden, and our proactive
financial management approach have all contributed to the City’s positive credit rating.
Despite these strengths, the City is not immune to the economic challenges discussed in the
previous section. In addition to the macro-economic challenges, there are factors specific to
Florida and the City of Hollywood that must be monitored to ensure the long-term fiscal
sustainability of the City. A few of these factors include:
• Ongoing affordable housing shortages
• The indirect effect of Hurricane Ian and other climate change events
• Ongoing and unprecedented labor shortages
• Unfunded mandates and other state legislation further limits local government’s ability
to generate additional revenues
• The cooling down effect in the real estate market and
• High unfunded pension and Other-Post Employment Benefits (OPEB) liabilities

Looking Ahead
I am proud of the tremendous work of our staff in putting together this robust budget. As
previously stated, the FY 2023 Operating Budget and Capital Improvement Plan represents
a turning point for the City of Hollywood. The additional revenues generated from the
increase in taxable values allowed the City an opportunity to fund many long-awaited
initiatives. However, we must continue to seek ways to reduce our reliance on property tax
dollars by generating additional revenues from alternative sources. Simultaneously, we must
continue to look for opportunities to control costs and reduce long-term obligations.
Looking ahead, we will continue to seek creative approaches to enhancing the delivery of
City services and improve the overall quality of life for our residents and visitors.
Additional information and a video of the annual budget process can be found on the City’s
website at Budget & Performance Management | Hollywood, FL - Official Website
(hollywoodfl.org).
Respectfully submitted,
Dr. Wazir A. Ishmael

The City of Hollywood, Florida
It’s all about location and Hollywood has it – in the heart of South Florida between
Fort Lauderdale and Miami. As the 3rd largest city in Broward County, it’s easy to
understand why Hollywood is both a sought-after tourist destination and a perfect spot
for doing business. The City’s founder came to Florida in the 1920s to create a “dream
city” where people could “make a living and live while making it.”
Hollywood is at the crossroads for international trade with easy access to major
employment centers, key transportation routes and is readily accessible by sea, air and
land. The region’s primary transportation resources include Interstates 95 and 595, the
Florida Turnpike, Tri Rail commuter rail, and two major railroads, all of which run
through the City. Hollywood is adjacent to Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International
Airport and is the primary home to Port Everglades, one of the top cruise ports in the
world and one of the most active containerized cargo ports in the United States.
Port Everglades
Miami International Airport and the Port of Miami are less than 25 miles away,
providing additional opportunities for Hollywood residents and companies to access the
global marketplace.

FY 2023 Operating Budget Initiatives
The City of Hollywood’s operating budget initiatives provide a short-term focus
to guide the development of the FY 2023 Operating Budget. In recent years, the City
has accomplished many of its fiscal goals, including the adoption of a structurally
balanced budget, maintaining its fund balance target and increasing its tax base. In
the immediate term, the City continues to take steps to ensure a structurally balanced
budget continues in future years. The Adopted FY 2023 budget incorporates strategies
that support this goal and guide sustainable financial decision making.

Revenue Initiatives
The City has certain policies, practices, and procedures in place in accordance
with industry best practices. Examples of these policies, practices, and procedures
include:

• Maintain an updated Comprehensive Schedule of Fees which compiles Citywide

revisions made to City fees, and facilitates an accessible history of all fees.
Property Tax Revenues
On July 1, 2022, the Broward County Property Appraiser’s Office certified the
City’s taxable property value at $21,001,968,219 which is $2,175,928,832 or 11.56%
above the previous tax year’s final gross taxable value, after corrections, of
$18,826,039,387. For FY 2023, the City adopted an operating millage rate of 7.4665
mills which is a decrease of 0.0145 mills below the previous year’s millage rate of
7.4810 mills. This operating millage rate is projected to generate $149,105,636 in net
property tax revenues for FY 2023, inclusive of statutory discounts, uncollectible taxes,
delinquent taxes, and penalties.
Fire Rescue Assessment Revenues
The Fire Rescue Assessment Fee will increase from $299 to $304 per residential
unit. The FY 2023 assessment covers 95% of the assessable cost of fire rescue services
provided to City residents and businesses, generating net revenues of approximately
$29.8 million.
Fire Inspection Fee Revenues
The Fire Inspection Fee will decrease from $21 to $19 for inspected residential
units and is expected to generate approximately $2.7 million in revenue.

General Obligation Bond (G.O.B.) Capital Improvement Projects
In November 2004, voters approved a two-part referendum supporting the
issuance of a General Obligation Bond for Public Safety and Parks & Recreation capital
improvements. Prioritization and scheduling of projects, along with the initial
selection of design and engineering agencies for larger projects began in FY 2005.
In March 2019, voters approved three questions for the Hollywood on the GO
Bond which provided funding to improve public safety facilities, purchase technology
and equipment and improve the City’s parks and cultural facilities. The first tranche of
debt in the amount of $70 million was issued in the fall of 2019 and the second
tranche totaling almost $95 million was issued in the fall of 2022.
The required debt payments for the three bond issuances total approximately
$13.7 million for FY 2023 and will be funded by the voted debt service millage rate of
0.6883 mills, which is an increase of 0.2727 mills above the FY 2022 voted debt service
millage rate of 0.4156.
GASB 75 - Other Post-Employment Benefits (OPEB) Requirement
The line item for “OPEB Cost” was first included in the operating budget for
several departments after the implementation of GASB 45. It remains an important
component of the budget considering its extensive impact on the fiscal condition of
the City. Each Enterprise Fund (Water, Sewer, Stormwater, Golf, Sanitation, and Parking)
and Central Service Fund includes an expense line that represents the anticipated cost
of post-employment benefits other than pension costs, as determined by an
independent actuary. This change was initiated by the Government Accounting
Standards Board (GASB), the national organization that sets standards for
governmental accounting and reporting, to provide more complete information on

Operating Policies & Procedures
Budget Policies
The development of the Operating Budget is guided by the following policies:
• The primary budgetary objective is to provide the highest possible level of
service to the residents of Hollywood without impairing the City’s financial
condition.
• The budget must be balanced for each fund; total projected revenues must
equal total anticipated expenditures.
• The City will avoid making budgetary decisions that balance the current year’s
budget at the expense of meeting future years’ obligations.
• The City will maintain a budgetary control system to ensure adherence to the
budget and will prepare quarterly projection reports which will analyze actual
revenues and expenditures to budgeted amounts.
• Annual budgets are legally adopted for the general fund, proprietary funds, and
certain governmental funds.
• Laws and policies on limitations to revenue sources must be explicitly
addressed in the budget process.
Basis of Budgeting and Accounting
The annual budget, which represents departmental appropriations, is prepared
on the modified accrual basis utilizing encumbrance accounting. Encumbrances are
used to indicate the “intent to purchase goods or services,” which does not need to be
received for liabilities and expenditures to be recorded.
In brief, the modified accrual basis of accounting allows for revenues to be
recognized when susceptible to accrual (measurable and available). Measurable means
the amount of the transaction can be scientifically determined. Available means
collectible within the fiscal period or subsequently to be used to pay liabilities of the
current period.
The Enterprise Funds (Water, Sewer, Sanitation, etc.) on the other hand, are
budgeted on a full accrual basis. Expenses are recognized when incurred. Revenues
are recognized when they are obligated to the City. For example, water and sewer fees
are recognized as revenue when bills are issued.

Administrative Budget Transfers
Administrative budget transfers include transfers from one line item to another
within a department’s operating budget, within a capital project, or changes between
divisions within a department. More specifically:
• Budget transfer requests of $5,000 or less require approval by the department
head and authorization by the Director of Budget and Performance
Management and the Director of Financial Services; and
• Budget transfers between $5,001 and $50,000 require the City Manager’s
approval. The transfers, however, must be made within the same office or
department within a distinct fund. Where the budget transfer is the result of
the creation of accounts and transfer of funds necessitated by the creation or
amendment of a division within a department or the creation or amendment of
a special office within the Office of the City Manager, the City Manager may
approve the budget transfer without Commission action. All administrative
budget transfers are documented and tracked in the City’s financial
management system.
Commission Approved Budget Amendments
Budget amendments requiring City Commission approval are those amendments
which result in a change to the total appropriations of a department or fund and/or
exceed the City Manager’s approval threshold. The following amendments require
Commission approval
• All budget transfers over $50,000
• The acceptance and appropriation of a grant award
• The appropriation of additional funding if expenditures are projected to exceed
budgeted amounts
• Any budget transfers and adjustments within a sixty (60) day period following
the close of the fiscal year, per Florida Statute 166.241
• Adjustments to reflect unanticipated revenues or receipts; and
• Transfers of appropriation from one department or fund to another.
Budget Control
Budgetary control is maintained at the fund and department level. The
department level of control is the highest level in which management may reassign
resources without City Commission approval (within the threshold as established in
the City’s Comprehensive Budget Policy).

Overview of Funds
The City follows the Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) as part of
the budget and accounting processes. These principles require that the City use
separate accounting entities called funds to keep track of the resources used for
different purposes. There are two (2) different classes of funds: Governmental Funds,
which include the General Fund, special revenue funds, debt service funds and capital
projects funds; and Proprietary Funds, which include the enterprise funds and internal
service funds.
Governmental Funds
This category includes those individual funds through which most
governmental functions are typically financed.
General Fund (001) – The fund used to account for all financial resources, except
those that are required to be accounted for in another fund. Most governmental
operations such as police and fire protection, recreation activities, and planning and
zoning administration are accounted for in this fund. Ad Valorem property taxes are
also budgeted in this fund.
Special Revenue Funds (111, 113, 115, 116 and 164) – The City uses special
revenue funds, considered non-major governmental funds, to account for specific
revenues that are legally restricted to expenditure for specific purposes. These funds
relate primarily to revenues and expenditures associated with federal, state, and local
government grant programs. Expenditures for these purposes are dependent upon
receipt of related revenues.
Building Fund (114) – This special revenue fund is used to account for revenues and
expenditures related to enforcement of the Florida Building Code including review of
building plans, building inspections, and permit processing.
Debt Service Fund (223) – This fund is used to track the payment of principal and
interest related to long-term debt used by the City of Hollywood. These instruments of
debt may include special obligation bonds, bond anticipation notes, and tax
anticipation notes.
General Obligation Bond Fund (226) – This fund accounts for revenues from ad
valorem taxes and payment of principal and interest on the City’s General Obligation
Bonds.

Capital Projects Funds (333, 334 and 335) – Funds created to account for financial

Fund Balance / Net Position
Net position is the difference between an entity's assets plus deferred outflows
of resources and its liabilities plus deferred inflows of resources. Within the
governmental funds, this difference is known as fund balance. This accumulation of
unexpended revenues may be reserved to provide a financial cushion in the event of
unanticipated reductions in revenues, sudden economic downturn and to cover a
reasonable level of extraordinary unbudgeted expenditures.
The City maintains two (2) months of General Fund operating expenditures in
reserves (approximately 17%). This budgeted reserve target is a portion of the
unassigned fund balance that is reflected in the City’s annual financial report. The
City’s Annual Financial Reports may be found on the following page:
http://www.hollywoodfl.org/538/Annual-Financial-Pension-Fund-Reports.

Five-Year Forecast
The Five-Year Forecast is a tool used for long-range financial planning and
budgeting purposes. It is a series of estimates – informed estimates, but estimates,
nonetheless. Two primary goals of the forecast are to: (1) eliminate the structural
budget gap and (2) maintain reserves in the General Fund at the target level
of seventeen percent (17%) of total expenditures.
There are many variables involved in the development of a multiple
year financial forecast, and several assumptions for each of those variables, on
both the revenue and expenditure side. There are many unknown variables, for
example, the rate of future growth in the assessed valuation of taxable properties.
The forecasts project revenues and expenditures for the General Fund and
other major funds for FY 2023 through FY 2027. Sound financial management
requires that the City plan for the future by identifying potential issues in advance
and working to devise solutions rather than waiting for problems to evolve.
Based on the Five-Year Forecast presented in this document, there is one
valuable conclusion that can be drawn. The City, similarly to many local
governments, continues to face the challenge of the annual growth in expenditures
outpacing the annual growth in revenues. In recent years, the Office of Budget
& Performance Management (Budget Office) in collaboration with City Departments
and Offices have taken steps to improve the financial outlook of the City. A
Comprehensive Budget Policy was established to guide City Departments and
Offices in managing budgetary resources responsibly. Additionally, a
Comprehensive Schedule of Fees was established to provide a more organized
method for review and revision of fees during the annual budget process.
Furthermore, the Coronavirus Local Fiscal Recovery Fund, established under the
American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA), has been an instrumental resource following
the economic impacts stemming from the pandemic. Although a one-time resource,
the ARPA funds have helped the City to address critical needs that would have
otherwise increased projected gaps.
In summary, the collaborative efforts of City staff along with significant growth
in taxable values contributed to the City’s ability to produce a structurally
balanced budget in FY 2023. However, it is important to note that the volatility in
the current economic environment will inevitably challenge the City’s ability to sustain
a structural balance in the outyears. For these reasons, the five-year forecast
utilizes several external sources when developing the assumptions. Some of the
sources include the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the real Gross Domestic Product
(GDP) as projected by the Congressional Budget Office (CBO); Florida Department of
Revenue; the Office of Economic and Demographic Research; and Broward County
Property Appraiser. Major assumptions are detailed on the following pages.
